Nepal’s Luxury Tax

A New Chapter in Transparency & Compliance

Buying gold jewelry or checking into a five-star hotel? You might see a new 2% "luxury fee." We break down what this new invoice is, why it exists, and the debates it's sparking.

Understanding the Invoice

The Government of Nepal has prescribed a specific format for the Luxury Tax Invoice. It's designed to be transparent, ensuring the 2% fee is calculated and disclosed properly. Here’s what it includes:

1. Header Details

Includes invoice number, date, and PAN/Name/Address for both the seller and the buyer. It also specifies the payment method (cash, cheque, etc.).

2. Itemized Description

A detailed list of goods/services. For gold, this includes description, weight, and purity to clearly identify what's being sold.

3. Value Breakdown

This is key. It splits the value into non-taxable (e.g., old gold you trade in) and taxable (the new value added by the vendor). This prevents taxing your own gold.

4. Tax Calculations

Applies the 2% luxury tax to the taxable amount. If the item includes VAT-applicable parts (like a diamond), it also calculates the 13% VAT separately on that portion.

Important Note on Trade-ins:

Any jewelry or gold the customer brings in (a common practice) *must* be itemized on the invoice. This ensures transparency, even though that portion isn't taxed.

Why Was This Tax Introduced?

Introduced in Fiscal Year 2081/82 (2024 AD), the 2% luxury fee targets high-end consumption. The government positioned it as a strategic move to:

  • 1.

    Boost Revenue

    Tax high-end spending on services (five-star hotels, resorts) and goods (imported liquor, high-value jewelry).

  • 2.

    Increase Transparency

    By tracking high-value trades, the government aims to ensure big-ticket purchases aren't avenues for tax evasion.

  • 3.

    Exit the FATF Grey List

    A key goal is to curb illicit financial activity and money laundering, strengthening Nepal's financial integrity.

Initially, the fee for jewelry only applied to transactions over NPR 1 million. This threshold was later removed to stop businesses from splitting invoices to avoid the tax.

The Great Debate: Gold & Diamonds

The rollout was met with significant pushback, especially from gold and silver dealers.

Industry Protests

Dealers labeled the tax "unjust and impractical," staging street protests and shuttering shops in mid-2025, warning it would deepen the industry's crisis.

The 13% VAT on Diamonds

Alongside the luxury fee, a proposed 13% VAT on diamond-embedded jewelry caused an uproar. Jewelers feared this would make premium jewelry un-competitively expensive.

Fears of Smuggling

A key argument: If taxes make Nepali jewelry 10-13% more expensive, buyers will be driven to informal channels or across the border, unintentionally promoting smuggling.

Administrative Burden

The new policy requires detailed monthly tax returns, a significant compliance shift from previous annual filings, which businesses found onerous.

Where Things Stand Now

After the initial uproar, the government engaged with stakeholders and made several key adjustments to fine-tune the policy.

Key Clarifications & Adjustments:

Temporary Pause: In July 2025, enforcement was informally paused, and a task force was formed to review the "luxury" classification, getting shops to reopen.

B2B Exempt: A key change clarified that the 2% tax applies only at the final sale to a consumer. Business-to-business (B2B) transactions are exempt.

Buy-Backs Exempt: Sales from a private individual to a business (e.g., selling your old jewelry to a shop) are also exempt from the fee.

With these revisions, industry associations largely welcomed the government's decision, and the policy is now in full effect. Businesses must issue the proper invoice and file monthly returns, with penalties for non-compliance.

The Ongoing Outlook

The luxury tax is more than just paperwork; it's a balancing act between regulating luxury markets and supporting business growth.

The government will be watching revenue patterns and whether the policy successfully traces large transactions and reduces off-book trade. Meanwhile, businesses are adapting to the new compliance rules, marking a significant step toward greater fiscal transparency in Nepal's economy.
